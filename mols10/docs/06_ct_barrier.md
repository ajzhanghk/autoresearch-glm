# The Common Transversal Barrier: Why CT ≤ 2 for All Known MOLS-10 Pairs

## 1. The Central Obstruction

Every MOLS-10 pair $(L_1, L_2)$ generated by this project satisfies:

$$\mathrm{CT}(L_1, L_2) \leq 2$$

Since $n = 10$ and the CT Necessity Theorem requires $\mathrm{CT}(L_1, L_2) \geq 10$ for a third orthogonal square to exist, **no MOLS pair we have found can be part of a 3-MOLS triple**.

This section analyzes what is known about this barrier, its possible causes, and whether it is universal.

## 2. Common Transversal Counts for Specific Pairs

### Parker TV-21 Pair

The canonical MOLS-10 pair constructed by Parker (1960), here called TV-21, has:
- $\mathrm{CT}(L_1^{\text{TV}}, L_2^{\text{TV}}) = 2$

This was verified exhaustively by our enumeration algorithm. The two CTs were explicitly found; no additional CTs exist.

### Isotopy Variants of TV-21

Since CT is an isotopy invariant of the pair (when the same isotopy $\theta$ is applied to both squares simultaneously), all "same-isotopy" variants of TV-21 also have CT = 2.

**However:** Applying **different** isotopies to $L_1$ and $L_2$ separately generates new pairs not isotopically equivalent to TV-21 as a pair. These were tested:

| Method | Pairs tested | Max CT |
|--------|-------------|--------|
| Same isotopy (CT-invariant baseline) | All treated as 1 class | 2 |
| Different isotopies (L1 fixed, L2 varied via intercalate) | 311 pairs | **2** |
| Random L1 + multi_decomp L2 | 124 pairs | **2** |
| SA from random start | Many | **2** |

No method has found CT > 2 for any MOLS-10 pair.

## 3. Why Might CT ≤ 2 Hold Universally?

### Hypothesis (Strong Form)

> **Conjecture:** For all MOLS-10 pairs $(L_1, L_2)$, $\mathrm{CT}(L_1, L_2) \leq 2$.

If true, this would immediately imply $N(10) = 2$.

### Hypothesis (Weak Form)

> **Weaker Conjecture:** For all MOLS-10 pairs $(L_1, L_2)$, $\mathrm{CT}(L_1, L_2) < 10$.

This would also imply $N(10) = 2$ but allows CT values in $\{3, 4, \ldots, 9\}$.

### Algebraic Motivation

For Galois field constructions with prime power order $q$, MOLS pairs have CT = $q$ (the full complement). For $n = q$, $\mathrm{CT} = n$ means $N(n) \geq 3$ follows directly.

Order 10 has no Galois field structure. The available algebraic groups of order 10 are:
- $\mathbb{Z}_{10}$ (cyclic)
- $D_5$ (dihedral)

Neither of these produces MOLS (since 10 is not a prime power, the standard group-ring construction fails). The failure of these algebraic structures may explain why CT remains low.

### Connection to Intercalate Structure

An **intercalate** of $L_2$ (a $2 \times 2$ Latin subsquare) affects the common transversal structure when it "crosses" a transversal of $L_1$. Specifically:

> An intercalate flip on $L_2$ may create or destroy CTs by changing which symbol-pair assignments occur at 4 cells.

The sa_ct_climb algorithm exploits this: it tries intercalate flips and keeps those that increase CT. Despite extensive search, CT has never exceeded 2 via this method.

**Possible explanation:** The MOLS-10 constraint ($\mathrm{cl}_{12} = 0$) is so restrictive that any intercalate flip that maintains it also destroys any CT that might have been gained.

### Latin Square Order Constraints

For even orders $n$, not all Latin squares have orthogonal mates. The Hall-Paige condition (related to complete mappings) limits which groups can produce orthogonal Latin squares. For $n = 10$:

- The group $\mathbb{Z}_{10}$ satisfies Hall-Paige (since $10 = 2 \times 5$ and both factors satisfy it).
- However, the Cayley table of $\mathbb{Z}_{10}$ is not a Latin square orthogonal to itself.

This suggests a deep algebraic obstruction, though not a complete proof.

## 4. Evidence Against the CT Barrier Being Universal

While our search strongly supports CT ≤ 2, we cannot rule out:

1. **Unexplored isotopy classes:** The space of MOLS-10 pairs modulo isotopy is large. We have only sampled a fraction.

2. **Different construction methods:** Parker's TV-21 and its variants dominate our search. Completely different MOLS-10 constructions might produce pairs with higher CT.

3. **Larger search space:** 311 pairs is a very small sample of all MOLS-10 pairs. Millions of pairs would need to be tested for a definitive empirical conclusion.

## 5. SAT-Based Certification

The SAT solver provides **absolute certificates** of non-existence for individual pairs:

For each of our 311 pairs, Glucose3 returned UNSAT, meaning:
> **For this specific $(L_1, L_2)$ pair, no Latin square $L_3$ satisfies $L_3 \perp L_1$ and $L_3 \perp L_2$.**

This is stronger than the CT condition alone (which says "CT < 10 implies no L3") — the SAT certificate is valid regardless of the CT count.

### SAT vs CT

The SAT solver requires the full CNF encoding (~23,000 clauses) to reach the UNSAT conclusion. The CT condition provides a much faster certificate (enumeration takes a few seconds) but requires CT < n.

For our pairs, both methods agree: no L3 exists. The SAT certificate is independent confirmation.

## 6. Implications of the CT Barrier

### If CT ≤ 2 for ALL MOLS-10 Pairs

Then $N(10) = 2$ follows immediately, resolving one of the oldest open problems in combinatorics. This would be a major result.

### If Some MOLS-10 Pair Has CT ≥ 10

Then $N(10) \geq 3$ (a compatible L3 would exist by Algorithm X / exact cover). This would be an even bigger result.

### Current Status

We have not found evidence for either conclusion definitively. The CT barrier at 2 is strong empirical evidence for $N(10) = 2$, consistent with expert opinion, but the search space is too large for a complete computational proof.

## 7. The 12-Mate Analysis

We tested 12 distinct orthogonal mates of the TV-21 $L_1$ square (generated by multi_decomp):

- All 12 mates have $\mathrm{CT}(L_1^{\text{TV}}, L_2^{(i)}) = 2$
- The CTs themselves differ between mates (different specific cell sets)
- No mate achieves CT > 2

This suggests the CT barrier is not a fluke of TV-21 specifically — it appears for all mates of a given $L_1$.

## 8. Minimum Energy Under the CT Barrier

Given that CT ≤ 2 implies no perfect L3 exists, what is the minimum achievable $E$ for a triple $(L_1, L_2, L_3)$ where $(L_1, L_2)$ is a MOLS-10 pair?

**Observed:** $E_{\min} = 37$ (empirically, after extensive search).

**Theoretical lower bound:** The minimum $E$ over all triples with $\mathrm{cl}_{12} = 0$ must satisfy $E \geq 0$. For pairs with CT ≤ 2, we conjecture $E_{\min} \geq 2$ (at least one pair must appear 0 times and one must appear 2 times, contributing at least 2 to each of cl13 and cl23).

The gap between our lower bound (0 or 2) and the observed minimum (37) is large. Whether $E$ can be reduced below 37 remains open.
